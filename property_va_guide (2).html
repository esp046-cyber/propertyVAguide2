    Q: Do I need a license to be a Property VA?

    A: No, you don't need a real estate broker license to work as a VA. However, you cannot directly sell properties or sign contracts. You work under a licensed broker who handles the actual transactions. Your role is administrative and marketing support.

    Q: How long does it take to close a property deal?

    A: Timeline varies:
    - Cash transactions: 30-60 days
    - Bank financing: 60-90 days
    - In-house financing: 7-30 days
    Factors: document completeness, title verification, bank approval, buyer readiness

    Q: What if the buyer backs out after reservation?

    A: Depends on the reservation agreement:
    - Usually reservation fee is forfeited
    - Read the contract terms carefully
    - Some allow cancellation with penalty
    - Protect seller's interests while being fair
    - Always have written reservation agreements

    Q: Can foreigners buy property in the Philippines?

    A: Limited:
    - Cannot own land
    - CAN own condos (up to 40% of building can be foreign-owned)
    - Can own houses but not the land (long-term lease)
    - Can inherit land but must sell within reasonable time
    - Married to Filipino: land can be under spouse's name

    Q: What's the difference between TCT and CCT?

    A:
    TCT (Transfer Certificate of Title): For land and houses, shows lot ownership
    CCT (Condominium Certificate of Title): For condo units, shows unit ownership plus share in common areas
    Both are equally valid proof of ownership.

    Q: How do I handle multiple interested buyers?

    A: Best practice:
    - First come, first served (based on reservation with payment)
    - Be transparent with all parties
    - Set deadline for decision
    - Don't play buyers against each other (unethical)
    - Keep backup buyers warm in case primary falls through
    - Document everything

    Q: What's earnest money / reservation fee?

    A: Earnest money shows buyer's serious intent. Typical amounts:
    - ‚Ç±5,000 - ‚Ç±20,000 for lower-priced properties
    - ‚Ç±50,000 - ‚Ç±100,000 for mid-range
    - ‚Ç±100,000+ for high-end
    It's applied to the downpayment if deal proceeds, or forfeited if buyer backs out (per agreement).

    Q: Should I work with one broker or multiple?

    A: Depends on your arrangement:
    Exclusive (one broker): More support, training, steady work, but limited to their listings
    Multiple brokers: More opportunities, higher income potential, but less support and possible conflicts
    Start exclusive to learn, then expand when experienced.

    Q: How do I know if a listing is fake?

    - Can't verify identity

      ‚öñÔ∏è Understanding Philippine Property Laws

      Key Legal Principles

      • Foreign Ownership: Foreigners cannot own land, but can own condos (up to 40% foreign ownership per building)
      • Torrens System: PH uses title registration system for land ownership
      • Prescription Period: 10 years for ordinary, 30 years for extraordinary possession
      • Community Property: Property acquired during marriage is conjugal (both spouses own)

      üìã Types of Land Titles in PH

      Title Hierarchy (Best to Least Secure)

      1. TCT (Transfer Certificate of Title): Most secure, registered with Registry of Deeds, has owner's name
      2. CCT (Condominium Certificate of Title): For condo units, includes unit number and building
      3. OCT (Original Certificate of Title): First title issued for land
      4. Tax Declaration: NOT proof of ownership, only for tax purposes
      5. Deed of Sale: Transfer document, not title itself

      Red Flag: If seller only has Tax Declaration or Deed of Sale but no TCT/CCT, verify thoroughly with Registry of Deeds. Could be fake or disputed property.

      Verifying Authentic Titles

      • Check with Registry of Deeds in the city/municipality
      • Request Certified True Copy (CTC) of title
      • Verify owner's name matches seller's ID
      • Check for annotations (mortgage, liens, disputes)
      • Look for security features (watermark, seal)
      • Verify TCT number with Registry

      üö® Common Property Scams to Avoid

      Warning Signs

      Fake Titles: Photocopied titles, missing security features, wrong format
      Duplicate Selling: Property sold to multiple buyers
      Squatter Issues: Professional squatters claim ownership
      Boundary Disputes: Neighbors claiming overlap
      Estate Sales: Heirs not agreeing, fake heirs appearing
      Foreclosed Properties: Bank ownership not clear yet

      How to Protect Buyers

      1. Always verify title at Registry of Deeds
      2. Conduct ocular inspection with surveyor
      3. Check property with Barangay for issues
      4. Request tax clearance and receipts
      5. Hire lawyer for title investigation
      6. Insist on meeting at property location
      7. Never pay full amount before title transfer

      üí∞ Taxes & Fees in Property Transactions

      Buyer's Responsibilities (Purchase)

      • Notarial Fee: 1% - 2% of selling price

      Buyer typically pays: 3-5% of property value in taxes/fees
      Seller typically pays: 6-8% in capital gains tax and fees
